Final Research Project — The Premier League

The Beautiful Game's
Ugly Truth

The English Premier League is the world's most lucrative soccer competition — a global spectacle built on the athletic labor of Black and brown players. Yet the people who own, manage, and govern the game remain overwhelmingly white. This site argues that the Premier League's racial inequality is not incidental but structural: a direct product of the same hyper-commercialized system that makes the league so profitable.

Who profits from Black athletic labor in the Premier League?
Why does representation collapse above the pitch?
Can player activism produce genuine structural change — or is resistance absorbed by the market?

I
Section Two
02

The System: Money, Labor,
and Race

The Premier League generated over £6.7 billion in revenue in the 2022–23 season, driven by a broadcast rights model that distributes billions annually across twenty clubs. Yet this extraordinary wealth flows almost entirely to a club ownership class that is overwhelmingly white and, increasingly, foreign — American private equity consortia, Gulf sovereign wealth funds, and European billionaires. The question this raises is not merely aesthetic. It is structural: who benefits from whom?

The answer, as Ben Carrington argues in Race, Sport and Politics, lies in understanding the Premier League through the lens of racial capitalism — a system in which Black and brown athletic labor is commodified, valorized, and traded as a global asset class, while the governance and ownership structures that extract that value remain racially exclusive. The transfer market is the most visible expression of this logic. Players from West Africa, Brazil, and the Caribbean are scouted, purchased, and resold as appreciating assets. Their bodies generate revenue; their voices do not govern.

The chart above visualizes what EPL Equality Reports have documented for years: representation does not merely fall as you move up the hierarchy — it collapses. A league in which nearly half of all players identify as Black or minority ethnic produces barely one-in-fourteen managers of the same background, and a boardroom that is over 90% white. The Rooney Rule — requiring clubs to interview at least one minority candidate for senior coaching vacancies — was adopted in 2021. Its impact on actual hiring has been, at best, marginal.

The transfer market functions as a labor extraction mechanism: players are recruited young from the Global South, often by affiliated academies, and funneled into a European ownership structure they will never control.

Szymanski and Kuper's Soccernomics and Goldblatt's The Age of Football both document the Premier League's transformation into a genuinely global economic entity. But neither text fully reckons with the racial dimension of that transformation. Where Szymanski and Kuper celebrate the efficiency gains of global talent markets, Carrington forces us to ask: efficient for whom? The 2021 Parliamentary hearings on the proposed European Super League — in which club owners sought to further consolidate power at the expense of fans and smaller clubs — revealed just how far removed governance has become from the communities and labor that sustain it.

"Sport has become one of the key sites through which racial capitalism reproduces itself — extracting value from Black athletic bodies while maintaining white control over the institutions that govern them."

— Ben Carrington, Race, Sport and Politics (2010)

This is the argument that distinguishes this analysis from more conventional accounts of diversity in sport. The problem is not simply a pipeline issue — not merely a shortage of qualified Black coaches or executives. The problem is systemic: a financial architecture designed to maximize the value extracted from athletic labor while concentrating governance power among those who supply capital, not bodies.

II
Section Three
03

Case Studies &
Resistance

Structural arguments risk abstraction. Two cases put a human face on what the data shows — and reveal the gap between the image of inclusion the Premier League projects and the reality its players describe.

Case 01
Euro 2020: Racial Abuse & the Limits of Solidarity
Following England's penalty shootout defeat to Italy in the Euro 2020 final, Marcus Rashford, Bukayo Saka, and Jadon Sancho — the three players who missed their kicks — were subjected to a sustained campaign of racial abuse on social media. The abuse was swift, coordinated, and overwhelmingly directed at the team's Black players. The Football Association condemned it. The government condemned it. Social media platforms promised reviews. And within weeks, the conversation had moved on — with no meaningful platform accountability and no structural reform of how the FA or clubs handle online racial abuse. The episode illustrated a recurring pattern: institutional condemnation without institutional change.
Case 02
Raheem Sterling & the Politics of Speaking Out
Raheem Sterling has been the Premier League's most sustained and articulate critic of institutional racism — targeting not only fans but the media itself. In 2018, he publicly called out the Daily Mail and other tabloids for racially coded coverage: describing his white teammates' tattoos as aspirational while framing his own identically as gangster imagery. His intervention sparked a national debate and, unusually, produced concrete results: the Daily Mail amended its coverage policies. Sterling also used his platform during the BLM movement of 2020, advocating for structural change rather than symbolic gestures. His case is significant precisely because it demonstrates both the power and the precarity of player activism: effective within narrow limits, but dependent on individual courage rather than institutional support.

Institutional Responses: Reform or Performance?

The Premier League and its clubs have responded to racial inequality with a battery of visible initiatives. The question — posed most sharply by scholars like Carrington — is whether these constitute structural reform or what sociologists call performative tokenism: symbolic action that manages the appearance of change while leaving underlying power structures intact.

The contrast is sharp. Taking the knee became a ritual emptied of political content — initially meaningful, then contested by sections of the fanbase, then defended not as an anti-racist statement but as a "personal choice," before being quietly phased out by most clubs by 2022. The Rooney Rule has produced interview diversity without hiring diversity. The overall picture is of a league that has learned to speak the language of inclusion while maintaining the structures of exclusion.

"I'm not the one who should have to fix this. I just want to play football. But if I don't say something, who will?"

— Raheem Sterling, The Players' Tribune (2020)
III
Section Four
04

Conclusion & Sources

This site has argued that racial inequality in the Premier League is not a residual problem — a legacy of historical prejudice being slowly resolved by market forces and diversity initiatives. It is a structural feature of the league's political economy. The same system that generates billions from the global transfer of Black and brown athletic labor actively concentrates ownership, management, and governance in the hands of a predominantly white capital class.

"The market does not solve racial inequality in sport — it produces it. Commodifying Black athletic excellence and excluding Black institutional power are not contradictions within the system; they are the system."

Where scholars like Szymanski and Kuper focus on market efficiency and Goldblatt on the geopolitics of global soccer, this analysis insists that we cannot understand the Premier League's political economy without understanding its racial economy. Carrington's framework of racial capitalism — developed in a sporting context — remains the most useful theoretical tool for this purpose, and the EPL equality data, transfer records, and player testimonies gathered here provide the empirical evidence that confirms it.

What would genuine equity look like? It would require not just diverse interview shortlists but diverse hiring outcomes; not just symbolic anti-racism campaigns but enforceable accountability for online abuse; not just player statements but institutional governance that reflects the communities whose labor sustains the game. The evidence presented here suggests that such equity cannot be achieved through reform of the current commercialized system — it would require challenging the concentration of ownership and financial power that defines the Premier League as an institution.

Whether that challenge will come from players, fans, or regulators — or whether the market will continue to absorb and neutralize resistance — remains the defining question for the next chapter of the beautiful game.
